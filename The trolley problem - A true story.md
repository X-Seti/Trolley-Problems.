# THE TROLLEY PROBLEM: A COMPLETE DISASTER
## *The Real Story, From Every Perspective*

---

## PROLOGUE: THE SITUATION

Three tracks diverge from the main line.

**Track 1:** A passenger train approaching, five minutes out. Beyond the tracks, an 8-lane highway separates the railway from residential areas, schools, and parks.

**Track 2:** Under repair. Railway workers replacing sleepers and rails. Sections of track missing entirely.

**Track 3:** Appears clear, but just past a road crossing, a school bus has broken down. The crossing gates are stuck down. Children's voices can be heard playing nearby.

A driverless freight train barrels down the main line. No brakes. No stopping.

Someone has to choose which track it takes.

And in the middle of all this chaos, a baby has gone missing.

## PERSPECTIVE 1: MARCUS (The Blind Father)

**4 Minutes, 37 Seconds Until Impact**

Marcus's hands swept across the gravel between the tracks, his white cane tapping rhythmically. Left, right, forward. The pattern he'd perfected over forty years of blindness.

"David? Thomas? Anything?" His voice was steady, but his heart hammered against his ribs.

"Nothing yet," David called from somewhere to his left. "She was right here. RIGHT here. How far could she have crawled in thirty seconds?"

Thirty seconds. That's all it took. Marcus had bent down to tie his shoe. David and Thomas were three feet away discussing the train schedule. And little Emma - ten months old, curious as a cat, fast as lightning when properly motivated - had simply... vanished.

"The tracks curve here," Thomas shouted. "Maybe she went that way?"

Marcus's cane found the rail. Cold steel under his fingers. He followed it, calling softly. "Emma? Emma, sweetie, where are you?"

A stranger's voice cut through his concentration. Panicked. Male. Out of breath.

"You need to get OFF the tracks! There's a train coming!"

"We can't leave!" Marcus snapped. "My daughter is missing!"

"I understand that, but there's a TRAIN—"

"Then help us FIND HER!" David's voice, sharp with fear.

Marcus heard the stranger's footsteps falter. A muttered curse. Then: "Where did you last see her?"

"We didn't SEE her," Marcus said, trying to keep the bitterness from his voice. "We're blind. We HEARD her. She was babbling, right between the tracks. Then she went quiet."

"How long ago?"

"Maybe a minute. Maybe two."

The stranger made a sound like someone who'd just been punched. "Okay. Okay. I'll look. But you three need to move to the side—"

"NO!" All three of them shouted it simultaneously.

Marcus felt his hands trembling as they swept the ground. Emma loved trains. The vibrations. The rumbling sounds. What if she'd crawled ONTO the tracks? What if she was lying there, silent, and they couldn't find her because they couldn't SEE—

His cane struck something soft. His heart stopped.

"I found something!"

He dropped to his knees, hands reaching—

A backpack. Someone's abandoned backpack.

"Damn it!"

Behind him, the stranger was shouting something about a train in four minutes. Four minutes. Four minutes to find Emma or—

Marcus couldn't think about the "or."

"Emma!" he screamed. "EMMA!"

Only the wind answered.

And beneath it, so faint he almost missed it... was that a train whistle?

## PERSPECTIVE 2: EMMA (The Baby)

**4 Minutes, 12 Seconds Until Impact**

Daddy's shoelace had a weird metal bit at the end. Shiny. Emma liked shiny things.

She'd reached for it, but Daddy moved his foot. So Emma did what any reasonable baby would do: she crawled after it.

Then she saw something EVEN BETTER.

A butterfly! Orange and black! It fluttered past her face, and Emma squealed with delight. The butterfly flew away from the boring train tracks where Daddy and Uncle David and Uncle Thomas were talking about adult things.

Emma followed.

The butterfly was FAST. It went over the rocks (Emma's knees didn't like the rocks, but she was DETERMINED), past the big metal rail things, and toward—

Oh! Big road! Emma knew roads. Cars went on roads. Vrooooom!

The butterfly flew right over the road.

Emma paused at the edge. The road was LOUD. And WIDE. And cars were going SUPER fast.

But the butterfly...

A gap in the traffic. Emma's baby brain didn't calculate odds or assess risk. It just thought: BUTTERFLY!

She crawled.

Fast fast fast across the hot concrete. A car horn BLARED. Brakes SQUEALED. Emma didn't understand why the cars were so angry. She was just following the butterfly!

Made it across! The grass on the other side was soft and cool. Emma giggled.

The butterfly landed on a flower near a big fence. Emma crawled toward it—

BZZZZZZZZZ.

The fence made a scary humming sound. Emma didn't like it. The butterfly flew along the fence line, away from the buzzing.

Emma followed at a safe distance.

The fence went on forever. Emma's knees were getting tired. But look! There was a HOLE in the fence! A big hole, maybe 40 meters down, where someone had cut the chain-link.

The butterfly flew through the hole.

Emma crawled through behind it.

On the other side was a building with LOTS of humming. Even louder than the fence! And there were puddles! Emma liked puddles!

She crawled toward the nearest puddle—

It was green. And it smelled funny. And when Emma stuck her finger in it, it tingled in a way that made her pull back quickly.

Owie! Not water!

The butterfly landed on a rock next to an even bigger puddle. This one was orange and glowing and very very warm.

Emma decided maybe she didn't need to catch the butterfly after all.

Behind her, someone was shouting. Lots of people were shouting.

Emma looked back. Daddy? Uncle David? Uncle Thomas?

No. Different people.

Emma decided she'd had enough adventure. She wanted her daddy.

She started crawling back toward the hole in the fence, whimpering.

The orange puddle bubbled. It smelled like burnt hair and Bad Things.

Emma crawled faster.

## PERSPECTIVE 3: SARAH CHEN (The Teacher)

**3 Minutes, 48 Seconds Until Impact**

Sarah stood on the steps of the broken-down school bus, one hand gripping the safety rail, the other shading her eyes against the afternoon sun.

This was not how field trips were supposed to go.

Twenty-three third-graders behind her, getting restless. The bus had died right on the railroad crossing - of all the possible places to break down, it picked the ONE spot that made every safety coordinator in the district have nightmares.

She'd already called dispatch. Tow truck was "on its way." The crossing gates were malfunctioning - stuck in the down position. And now she was watching some kind of disaster unfold fifty meters away on the tracks.

Three men - blind, from the way they moved with canes - were frantically searching the area between the tracks. A fourth man, younger, was running back and forth like his hair was on fire, shouting about a train.

And there WAS a train. Sarah could see it in the distance. Still far away, but coming fast. Too fast.

"Miss Chen?" One of her students, Jamie, tugged at her jacket. "What are those men doing?"

"Stay on the bus, Jamie."

"But Miss Chen—"

"Stay. On. The. Bus."

Sarah was calculating distances. The men were maybe fifty meters up the track. The train was... she couldn't tell exactly. A kilometer? Less?

She pulled out her phone. No signal. Of course. They were in a dead zone between the towers.

The young man - Sarah decided to call him Panicked Guy - was now physically trying to pull one of the blind men off the tracks. The blind man shoved him away.

"What are they DOING?" Sarah muttered.

Behind her, she could hear the kids pressing against the bus windows. Someone started crying. Little Emily, probably. Emily cried when the class hamster sneezed.

"Miss Chen, I'm scared," another voice.

"It's okay, sweeties. Everyone stay calm. Stay in your seats."

She was the adult. She had to project calm. Even though her heart was jackhammering and her mind was screaming that they were trapped on a railroad crossing with a malfunctioning bus and gates that wouldn't rise and a train that was definitely getting closer.

The tall fence behind the tracks blocked her view of what lay beyond. She could see houses in the distance. Parks. Schools. Normal suburban life, completely unaware of the chaos about to unfold.

The worker crew on Track 2 had noticed the commotion now. They'd stopped working and were standing in a cluster, pointing and shouting. One of them started running toward the switch point.

That's when Sarah realized: someone was going to have to choose which track the train took.

Track 1: Passenger train approaching from the other direction. Head-on collision.

Track 2: Workers. Missing rails. Derailment almost certain.

Track 3: Her bus. Her twenty-three students. Stuck on the crossing.

"Oh God," Sarah whispered.

"Miss Chen?" Jamie again. "Is the train going to hit us?"

She wanted to lie. To say everything would be fine. But she was five-foot-eight, wearing a short green skirt under her green jacket, standing on the steps of a broken bus on a railroad crossing, and she could SEE the train now. Actually see it. Not just a distant shape but a real, massive, unstoppable freight train.

"I need everyone to listen very carefully," she said, turning to face her students. Her voice was calm. Years of managing classrooms kicked in. "We're going to evacuate the bus in an orderly fashion. Single file. Out the front door. Away from the tracks. No running. No pushing."

"But the gates—"

"We're going UNDER the gates. Everyone follow me."

She stepped off the bus, legs trembling. The fence was behind them. Tall. Chain-link. They'd have to climb it or find a gate.

Behind her, twenty-three children began filing out. Some were crying now. Most looked terrified. A few, bless their hearts, looked excited because children were weird like that.

Sarah looked back at the men on the tracks. Panicked Guy had given up trying to move them and was now running in a wide circle, apparently searching for something himself.

What were they looking for?

The train whistle blew. Long. Loud. Urgent.

**3 minutes, 12 seconds until impact.**

Sarah started herding her students toward the fence, looking for a gate, a gap, anything.

And that's when she saw it.

Forty meters down the fence line. A hole. Cut chain-link. Big enough for a person to squeeze through.

And just beyond it, near some kind of electrical substation...

Was that a BABY?

## PERSPECTIVE 4: CARLOS (Railway Worker)

**2 Minutes, 57 Seconds Until Impact**

Carlos dropped his wrench when he heard the shouting.

Track 2 was a mess. They'd been working on it for three days now - replacing rotten sleepers, pulling up bad rails, surveying for structural problems. Half the track was literally gone. Any train that came down this line would derail instantly.

Which is why the line was supposed to be CLOSED.

But now he was hearing a train whistle. And seeing a train. A BIG train. Coming FAST.

"Jesus, Mary, and Joseph," he breathed.

"Carlos!" His foreman, Miguel, was running toward him. "The main line switch - someone needs to—"

"On it!"

Carlos sprinted toward the switch point. The manual override was fifty meters away, near where the tracks diverged. He could see the mechanism from here. It was currently set to send traffic down Track 1.

Which had an INCOMING PASSENGER TRAIN.

"We gotta send it to 2!" Miguel shouted behind him.

"Track 2's got NO RAILS!"

"Better than a head-on collision!"

Carlos's boots pounded against the gravel. His crew of seven was scattering - some running toward the switch, others just running AWAY from the tracks entirely.

Smart. If this freight train came down their track at full speed, the derailment would send debris flying like shrapnel.

He reached the switch mechanism. It was an old mechanical one, not computerized. You had to throw the lever manually. He grabbed it—

"WAIT!" Someone was screaming. That young guy who'd been trying to move the blind men. "There's a bus on Track 3! Kids on a bus!"

Carlos looked. Sure enough, school bus. Stuck on the crossing. Gates down. And kids - he could SEE kids climbing out of the bus.

"Mother of—"

Track 1: Passenger train, five minutes out. Maybe less.
Track 2: His crew, missing rails, certain derailment.
Track 3: School bus with kids evacuating.

"SOMEBODY MAKE A DECISION!" Miguel screamed.

Carlos's hand was on the lever. The freight train was close enough now that he could feel the vibration through the rails.

**2 minutes, 23 seconds.**

The three blind men were still on Track 1, searching for something. The young guy was running toward the electrical substation, pointing and shouting.

The teacher lady was herding kids toward the fence.

His crew was scattering.

And Carlos had to choose.

Three impossible options. No good answer. Just less-bad choices.

His radio crackled. "Carlos, this is dispatch. We've got the passenger train on Track 1 stopping. Emergency brake. They're not gonna make it, but they're trying. You gotta divert to 2 or 3."

Track 2: His crew could run. They had time. Maybe.

Track 3: Those kids were CLIMBING A FENCE. Slow. Too slow.

Carlos's hand tightened on the lever.

"God forgive me," he whispered.

## PERSPECTIVE 5: JONAS (Panicked Guy)

**2 Minutes, 8 Seconds Until Impact**

I've made a lot of questionable decisions in my life, but trying to coordinate a multi-track disaster rescue while three blind men refused to move was probably top five.

Maybe top three.

"THE BABY WENT TOWARD THE SUBSTATION!" I screamed for the fifth time.

"WE'RE NOT LEAVING WITHOUT HER!" David screamed back.

"I'M GOING TO GET HER! YOU THREE NEED TO MOVE!"

"HOW DO WE KNOW YOU'LL FIND HER?"

"HOW DO WE KNOW YOU WILL?"

Not my finest moment, arguing with blind men while a freight train bore down on us. But I was losing my mind.

I'd seen the baby. Ten months old, maybe. Crawled right past me while I was trying to explain train physics to her guardians. Just... crawled away. Following a butterfly. I watched her go across the highway - EIGHT LANES OF TRAFFIC - like she had a divine shield. Cars swerving, brakes screaming, and she just kept crawling.

Made it across. Through a hole in the fence. Into the substation.

Where there were acid collection pits from the battery systems.

And runoff channels that fed into the city's industrial waste management.

Which included a decorative lava lamp art installation some genius city planner thought was "aesthetically interesting" near power infrastructure.

The baby was currently investigating a glowing orange puddle that was definitely NOT water.

"I CAN SEE HER!" I shouted. "She's by the substation! I'm going to get her!"

"Then GO!" Marcus yelled.

"Not until you three GET OFF THE TRACKS!"

"WE'RE NOT—"

The train whistle blew. So loud my teeth rattled.

**1 minute, 52 seconds.**

Screw it.

I sprinted toward the fence. The hole was forty meters down. I could make it. Grab the baby. Get her back to her fathers. Then everyone could get off the damn tracks and we could all live happily ever after.

Simple plan.

As I ran, I could see the school bus teacher - green jacket, green skirt, looking calm but terrified - pushing kids through a different gap in the fence. Good. Smart. Get the kids clear.

The railway workers were having an animated argument at the switch point. I could see one guy's hand on the lever, frozen in indecision.

The three blind men were still searching, crawling on their hands and knees now, calling for Emma.

And the train...

The train was CLOSE.

I reached the hole in the fence, squeezed through, ran toward the substation.

Emma was NOT sitting by the orange puddle.

"Where—?" I spun around, panic rising.

There. Beyond the lava art installation. A gap in the perimeter fence. And beyond that...

"Oh, you have GOT to be kidding me."

A crocodile enclosure. Because of COURSE there was a zoo facility adjacent to the electrical substation. Why wouldn't there be?

And sitting in the MIDDLE of the enclosure, giggling and clapping her hands, was Emma.

Surrounded by four very large crocodiles.

Who appeared to be... Their tails were slapping the ground in rhythmic patterns. Their jaws were snapping at each other - not aggressively, but almost like they were gesturing. One kept lunging forward, then pulling back. Another was shaking its head repeatedly in what looked like disagreement.

They were arguing.

## PERSPECTIVE 6.5: THE CROCODILES (Bruce, Gary, Derek, and Trevor)

**2 Minutes, 15 Seconds Until Impact (But The Crocodiles Don't Care About That)**

"Rock, paper, scissors, SHOOT!" Bruce threw down his... well, crocodiles don't have hands, so this was more of a tail-slap gesture that indicated his choice.

Rock.

"BRUCE!" Gary's jaw snapped in frustration. "You threw BEFORE 'shoot'! That doesn't count!"

"I did NOT!"

"Yes you DID! Trevor, back me up here!"

Trevor, the oldest and largest of the group, sighed with the weariness of someone who'd been having this argument for forty-five years. "I wasn't looking. I was watching the small human."

"TREVOR!"

"Well, it's not every day a tiny human just crawls into our enclosure! It's fascinating! Look at it - it has no survival instincts whatsoever. Just sitting there. Gurgling."

Emma waved at Trevor. Trevor, despite being a apex predator, found this oddly endearing.

"Can we FOCUS?" Derek snapped. "We're trying to determine who gets first bite, and Bruce keeps cheating!"

"I'M NOT CHEATING! You lot just don't understand the rules!"

"The rules," Gary said with dangerous calm, "have been the SAME since 1987 when we instituted the Rock-Paper-Scissors Protocol for meal distribution. You throw ON 'shoot', not BEFORE 'shoot'!"

"I thought we changed that in '94?"

"WE NEVER CHANGED IT!"

"Pretty sure we changed it during the Great Ibis Incident—"

"The Great Ibis Incident was about whether birds counted as 'fast food' or 'slow food' for rotation purposes! It had NOTHING to do with RPS timing!"

Emma had found a pebble and was trying to eat it. The crocodiles paused their argument to watch.

"Should we... stop it?" Derek ventured.

"It's not our problem what the human eats," Bruce said. "Now, can we PLEASE restart the game? I'm getting hungry."

"Fine. FINE." Gary positioned himself. "Rock, paper, scissors, shoot—"

"WAIT." Derek held up a claw. "Are we doing best-of-three or sudden death?"

Collective groan.

"We ALWAYS do sudden death for random encroachment meals!" Bruce said.

"No, we do best-of-three for scheduled feedings and sudden death for PLANNED encroachments. This was UNPLANNED!"

"Unplanned encroachments fall under the Random Distribution Protocol, paragraph 7, subsection C, which CLEARLY states—"

"Oh for the love of—" Trevor's massive tail slammed the ground. "The human is RIGHT THERE. It's not going anywhere. Can we just PLAY?"

"Only if we agree on the rules!"

"Best of three!"

"Sudden death!"

"BEST OF THREE!"

Emma had given up on the pebble and was now clapping her hands, delighted by the angry crocodile sounds.

"Look," Gary said, trying to be reasonable, "Derek has a point. The Protocol is ambiguous on unplanned encroachments. Paragraph 7 could be interpreted either way depending on whether you consider 'random' to mean temporally random or categorically random—"

"OH MY GOD, JUST THROW!" Bruce roared.

"Don't take the Lord's name in vain," Trevor muttered.

"YOU'RE A CROCODILE! YOU DON'T EVEN BELIEVE IN—"

"Rock, paper, scissors, SHOOT!"

Bruce: Rock
Gary: Paper  
Derek: Scissors
Trevor: Rock

"DEREK WINS!" Gary announced.

"WAIT!" Bruce protested. "I call foul! Derek threw SCISSORS again! You can't throw scissors three times in a row!"

"THERE'S NO RULE AGAINST THAT!"

"It's unsportsmanlike conduct!"

"IT'S RANDOM CHANCE, BRUCE!"

"It's BORING is what it is! You're using a pattern! That defeats the purpose of RPS!"

Derek's jaw dropped. "I'M using a pattern?! You threw ROCK four times yesterday during the duck incident!"

"That was DIFFERENT!"

"How was that DIFFERENT?!"

Emma had crawled about a meter closer to Trevor. Trevor looked down at her. She looked up at him with big, innocent eyes.

"Gah," Trevor said. "It's... it's kind of cute. A ready meal!"

"DON'T GET ATTACHED!" the other three shouted in unison.

"I'm not getting attached! I'm just saying, objectively, as a predator assessing prey, this particular prey is... aesthetically pleasing in its helplessness."

"Trevor's going soft," Bruce muttered.

"I AM NOT GOING SOFT! I'm just— LOOK, can we restart? This round was clearly compromised."

"By WHAT?"

"By Bruce's accusations throwing off the fairness of the outcome!"

"Oh, NOW we're worried about fairness?!"

Emma had reached Trevor and was patting his snout with her tiny hand.

"Goo," she said.

"See?" Trevor said weakly. "It makes sounds. Probably means something in human language."

"Probably means 'please eat me, I'm delicious'," Bruce said.

"That's not— that's a terrible translation—"

"RESTART!" Derek bellowed. "Rock, paper, scissors—"

And that's when Jonas came crashing through the fence.

Jonas hit the crocodile enclosure fence at a dead run and immediately regretted every life choice that led to this moment.

Four crocodiles. FOUR. And they were all staring at Emma, who was patting the biggest one's snout like it was a golden retriever.

"EMMA!" Jonas screamed.

All four crocodile heads swiveled toward him.

"Oh great," one of them said. "Another one."

WAIT. DID THAT CROCODILE JUST—

"GET AWAY FROM HER!"

## PERSPECTIVE 6: THE TRAIN (Because Why Not)

**0 Minutes, 0 Seconds Until Impact**

The freight train did not think. It could not think. It was 200 tons of steel and cargo with no functioning brakes and no ability to process the moral implications of its trajectory.

It simply existed. And moved. And obeyed physics.

The switch clicked. The train followed the rails. Track 3.

Crossing ahead. Gates down. Yellow bus sitting precisely where no bus should ever sit.

Impact.

The bus exploded into fragments. Metal and glass and engine parts spinning away like shrapnel. The train's front car crumpled but held. Momentum carried it forward, dragging the destroyed bus beneath it, grinding it into the tracks.

The train continued for another 300 meters before the friction finally brought it to a halt.

Behind it: destruction.

Track 1: Clear. Three blind men and a baby, safe on the embankment.
Track 2: Clear. Seven railway workers, watching in horror but alive.
Track 3: Destroyed bus. No casualties. Kids safe beyond the fence.

The train sat silent, cooling metal ticking in the afternoon heat.

Emergency sirens began to wail in the distance.

And somewhere behind the fence, a baby cried for her daddy.

## EPILOGUE: THE AFTERMATH

**45 Minutes After Impact**

Marcus sat on the back of an ambulance, Emma clutched in his arms. She'd been checked by paramedics. Minor scratches from the substation area. Nothing serious. She was giggling now, the trauma already forgotten in the way only babies can forget.

"She's okay?" Jonas asked. The panicked guy had turned out to be named Jonas. He looked exhausted.

"She's perfect," Marcus said. His voice was hoarse from screaming. "Thank you. I... we couldn't have found her. We TRIED, but—"

"You were looking for your baby," Jonas said. "Of course you didn't want to move."

David and Thomas stood nearby, hands on each other's shoulders. They hadn't let go of each other since the impact.

"We should have trusted you," David said quietly. "When you said the train was coming. We should have—"

"You were terrified," Jonas interrupted. "I get it. I really do."

Across the scene, Sarah Chen was doing headcount for the fifteenth time. Twenty-three kids. All present. All safe. Some were crying. Some were laughing with the manic energy of survived trauma. All were alive.

The bus was scrap metal scattered across a hundred meters of track.

"Anyone hurt?" a police officer asked her.

"No," Sarah said. "Everyone got out. Just in time."

She looked over at Jonas. Caught his eye. Mouthed: "Thank you."

He nodded back.

Carlos, the railway worker, sat on the ground with his head in his hands. Miguel stood next to him.

"You made the right call," Miguel said.

"Did I? I sent a train into a school bus."

"An EMPTY school bus. Everyone was already out."

"But I didn't KNOW that when I threw the switch. I just... there was no good option. Track 1 had the blind guys and the baby. Track 2 had us and would've derailed. Track 3 had kids, but they were evacuating, and maybe, MAYBE they'd make it in time."

"They did make it."

"By thirty seconds," Carlos said bitterly. "Thirty seconds the other way and I'd have killed twenty-three children."

Miguel had no answer to that.

**2 Hours After Impact**

The news crews arrived. They tried to interview Marcus ("The blind father whose baby's disappearance nearly caused a tragedy"). They tried to interview Sarah ("The heroic teacher who saved her students"). They tried to interview Carlos ("The railway worker who made the impossible choice").

All of them declined.

Jonas, sitting on a curb drinking water from a bottle someone had given him, watched the circus with exhausted detachment.

"Quite a day," said a voice next to him.

He looked up. One of the police officers. Older guy. Kind eyes.

"Yeah," Jonas said.

"You know what strikes me about all this?" the officer continued. "Everyone tried to do the right thing. The blind men looking for their baby. You trying to save everyone. The teacher protecting her kids. The railway worker making an impossible choice. Nobody was the villain here. Just people facing an impossible situation."

"The trolley problem," Jonas muttered.

"What?"

"Philosophy thought experiment. Train coming down the tracks. You can pull a lever to divert it, but every option kills someone. There's no right answer. Just different ways to fail."

The officer considered this. "Except here, nobody died."

"By luck. By thirty seconds of luck."

"Maybe. Or maybe by a lot of people doing their best in an impossible situation. Maybe that's the real answer to the trolley problem - not pulling a lever, but everyone working together to make sure fewer people die than would have otherwise."

Jonas looked at him. "That's very optimistic."

"I've been a cop for thirty years. I'll take my optimism where I can get it." He stood up. "You did good today, son. You saved that baby. And you helped save everyone else just by being there, coordinating, keeping people informed. That matters."

He walked away, leaving Jonas sitting on the curb.

Emma's giggles carried across the scene. Marcus was making funny faces at her. She was laughing. Safe. Alive.

Twenty-three kids were reunited with their parents. Crying. Hugging. Safe. Alive.

Seven railway workers were filling out incident reports. Shaken. Traumatized. But safe. Alive.

Jonas pulled out his phone. Started typing a message to his friend Claude.

*"Had an interesting day. Want to hear about a trolley problem that actually happened?"*

## THE REAL MORAL

There is no clean answer to the trolley problem. Not in philosophy. Not in real life.

Sometimes you're the person pulling the lever, knowing someone will die either way.

Sometimes you're the blind father searching for your baby, unable to perceive the danger around you.

Sometimes you're the baby, innocently crawling from one disaster to another, unaware of the chaos you're causing (or the crocodiles debating your edibility).

Sometimes you're the teacher, protecting those in your care while watching others face impossible choices.

Sometimes you're the worker, hand on the lever, seconds to decide, no good options.

And sometimes you're the panicked guy in the middle, trying to coordinate a disaster while everyone around you makes perfectly reasonable decisions that happen to conflict with everyone else's perfectly reasonable decisions.

The trolley problem isn't about math. It's about the moment when all your options are terrible and you have to choose anyway.

It's about the thirty seconds between "everyone might die" and "somehow, miraculously, everyone lived."
